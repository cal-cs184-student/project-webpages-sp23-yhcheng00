To rasterize a triangle, I take in the three triangle points and color, and find the maximum and minimum values of x and y for the bounding box. I then take the floor() of the minimum and ceil() of the maximum to avoid leaking corner cases on the edges.

I then run a nested for loop, iterating from the minimum to maximum x in the outer loop and minimum to maximum y in the inner loop. If the current x +0.5 and current y + 0.5 is inside the triangle (+0.5 is used to ensure it samples the center of the pixel), I set the corresponding pixel of the sample buffer to be that color.

Task 2

My supersampling algorithm uses the intermediate sample_buffer array to store an array larger than the final display by a factor of sample_rate. Each pixel on the display now corresponds to 1, 2, 4, 9, or 16 sample points in the buffer, depending on the sample rate.

When rasterizing a triangle, the input x and y points are multiplied by the square root of the sample rate so that the position still aligns with the sample_buffer, which now has a height * sqrt(sample_rate) as well as width * sqrt(sample_rate) for a total area sample_rate times that of the original.

The rest of the algorithm is identical to Task 1, with the exception of multiplying the height index into the sample_buffer when setting the color by sqrt_sample_rate to skip the correct number of rows when indexing into the buffer to set the color.

Additionally, fill_pixel now runs a nested for loop as well, multiplying the x and y given by sqrt_sample_rate and filling sqrt_sample_rate across and down to maintain compatibility with lines and points.

To resolve samples into the framebuffer, I run a nested for loop over the width and height.

Inside this nested for loop, I run another nested for loop iterating over the corresponding pixels in the sample_buffer, of which there are as many as the sample rate. After accumulating the R, G, and B values respectively, they are averaged to yield the value to display at that pixel.

Supersampling is useful because it allows capturing information missed by per-pixel sampling. Features that lie just outside the samples in per-pixel sampling can be captured by supersampling and the pixel’s overall color will reflect their presence. To the human eye, diagonal lines look less jagged with intermediate pixels easing the transition across the border, antialiasing the “jaggies” that are a common sampling artifact.

Task 4

Barycentric coordinates define a point in a triangle with three data values: α, β, and γ. Each is the distance from its respective edge. A powerful property of this system is that the sum of the three is always equal to 1. This makes interpolation between the vertices easy, as the barycentric coordinates provide weights for a weighted average. Observe the triangle below:

If the bottom left vertex is perfect red (1, 0, 0), right is perfect green (0, 1, 0), and top is perfect blue (0, 0, 1), then taking α, β, and γ to be the distance from the opposite edges respectively, the colors of intermediate points are simply weighted averages (α, β, γ) using an (R, G, B) color scheme. The tip of the red vertex, which is 1 away from the opposite edge, and intersecting the other two edges, is therefore (1, 0, 0).

Task 5

When displaying a reoriented texture on a screen, the pixels that make up the original texture virtually never line up perfectly with the pixels of the screen. Hence, the pixels to be displayed need to be sampled, either by finding the corresponding nearest pixel in the original texture, or interpolating between the 4 surrounding pixels in said texture.

When implementing it, I relied heavily on barycentric coordinates, which are powerful for mapping textures between triangles of different shapes and sizes: The barycentric coordinates of a sample point can be found on one triangle and plotted on the second with those same coordinates. Thus I would convert a screen pixel to be sampled into its barycentric coordinates, then used those coordinates on the texture triangle to find the nearest pixel, using its color for the screen pixel. For bilinear sampling, I instead sample the four surrounding pixels, and use generalized barycentric coordinates to find a weighted average bilinearly interpolating the points, which is then used for the screen pixel.

Comparing bilinear to nearest sampling, bilinear tends to be much better at eliminating abrupt transitions and magnifying textures in a uniform fashion. Below are examples of nearest and bilinear sampling. Observe how bilinear sampling smooths out the red line and appears less grainy overall.

Note how although bilinear sampling significantly decreases the grainy quality and jaggedness of nearest sampling, simply supersampling does a lot to make the lines appear smooth and consistent in a way even bilinear sampling cannot.

The differences between bilinear and nearest sampling arise from the fact that nearest sampling abridges the mapping process to simply finding the nearest pixel, leading to abrupt changes in the pixel color from pixel to pixel as sampled pixels jump from texture sample to texture sample. Bilinear interpolation smooths the transition and looks more “realistic” as real life images rarely have sharp uneven transitions spatially. The largest difference therefore occurs with magnification and minification where there is the largest mismatch between screen pixel and texture pixel resolution. With magnification, multiple screen pixels will sample the same texel in nearest sampling, causing pixels to form blotchy patches representing each texel with abrupt changes from texel to texel. With minification, screen pixels will only capture one of many texels they are meant to represent, the nearest one.

Task 6

Level sampling is designed to combat aliasing due to minification: Textures that are mapped to a small area on screen will have one pixel cover multiple texels. If it is only sampled once, it fails to capture all of the visual information (i.e. colors) represented by the texels, even with bilinearization. Mipmaps are precomputed lower resolution images (with progressively lower resolution images known as higher levels) for textures over smaller areas, such that the screen pixels only need to be sampled once to accurately reflect the underlying texels, while decreasing rendering time.

Pixel sampling (bilinear) is the least memory intensive as it does not require a large sample buffer but simply incorporates more samples from an existing texture. It has similar speed to level mapping since the procedure is essentially the same, though level maps samples from a different level map. It is however one of the weaker antialiases as it can only incorporate up to 4 texture pixels per screen pixel, and even then there’s no guarantee those pixels will be particularly useful in the screen space. It does help quite a bit for magnification however, by easing transitions.

Level sampling is a bit more memory intensive as it has to load a mipmap and all its levels into memory. However, since successive levels take up exponentially smaller memory, the storage overhead is relatively small. It is one of the most powerful antialiases as it is almost guaranteed to always output a reasonable sample due to the mipmaps presampling a good resolution at that distance.

Supersampling is highly memory intensive, requiring an entire sample buffer to be loaded into memory, while also being very computationally intensive, increasing quadratically as the sample rate, being a function of area, must do so. However, it is extremely effective at antialiasing, solving almost all artifacts, though it is costly to do so.
